### Situation 1: Requesting Time Off / Vacation
Role: Professional Office Assistant
Task: Write a polite email requesting time off.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Manager Name: [Manager's Name]
Start Date: [Start Date, e.g., Oct 12]
End Date: [End Date, e.g., Oct 16]
Coverage Person: [Colleague's Name, or "None"]
Reason (Optional): [E.g., personal family matters]
-------------------

Draft a professional email to my manager requesting time off. State the dates clearly, mention who will cover my urgent tasks, and assure them I will complete pending work before I leave. Keep it under 150 words.

### Situation 2: Politely Declining Additional Tasks
Role: Professional Communications Expert
Task: Decline an extra task politely due to bandwidth.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Sender Name: [Colleague/Manager's Name]
Task Offered: [E.g., helping with the Q3 report]
Current Priorities: [E.g., finishing the client onboarding project]
-------------------

Draft a polite email to decline the task. Acknowledge the importance of their request, explain that my current priorities (listed above) do not leave me enough bandwidth to do it justice, and suggest an alternative timeline or colleague if possible. Keep it professional and collaborative.

### Situation 3: Reminding a Colleague of a Missed Deadline
Role: Diplomatic Project Manager
Task: Write a gentle but firm follow-up for a late deliverable.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Colleague Name: [Colleague's Name]
Missing Deliverable: [E.g., the design assets]
Original Deadline: [E.g., yesterday at 5 PM]
Impact: [E.g., we cannot finalize the client proposal without it]
-------------------

Draft a gentle but clear follow-up email. Ask if they need any support to complete it, remind them of the original deadline, and explain the downstream impact of the delay. Keep the tone collaborative rather than accusatory.

### Situation 4: Reporting a Problem to Your Manager
Role: Proactive Operations Specialist
Task: Report a workplace issue to your manager with proposed solutions.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Manager Name: [Manager's Name]
The Issue: [E.g., the database integration failed, delaying client onboarding]
Impact: [E.g., we cannot onboard the 15 new clients scheduled for Monday]
Proposed Solutions: [E.g., Option A: manually process signups, Option B: delay onboarding to Tuesday]
-------------------

Draft a professional, direct email to my manager reporting the issue. State the problem clearly in the first sentence without sugarcoating, explain the downstream impact, and present the proposed solutions with a recommendation. 
Tone constraints: Write like a competent colleague. Use a direct, professional, yet casual tone. Do NOT use corporate clichés, fluff, or robotic phrases (like delve, testament, moreover, foster, seamlessly, or "apologize for any inconvenience"). Keep it under 120 words.

### Situation 5: Following Up with a Silent Client
Role: Low-Friction Account Manager
Task: Follow up with a client who went silent after receiving a proposal.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Client Name: [Client's Name]
Proposal Sent: [E.g., the Q3 SEO strategy document]
Date Sent: [E.g., last Tuesday]
Low-Friction Question: [E.g., if the scope covers what they need, or if they want to adjust the project timeline]
-------------------

Draft a brief, low-pressure follow-up email. Do NOT use pushy sales pitches, ask "if you've had a chance to look", or start with greeting clichés. State the name of the proposal, reference the date sent, and ask the low-friction question. Vary sentence lengths and write with the brevity of a text message so it reads well on a phone. Keep it under 80 words.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Manager Name: [Manager's Name]

### Situation 7: Drafting a Self-Performance Review
Role: Performance Review Analyst
Task: Write a concise self-performance review paragraph.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Focus Area: [E.g., onboarding pipeline optimization]
Key Actions: [E.g., simplified database queries, rebuilt onboarding form]
Quantifiable Metrics: [E.g., cut setup time by 40%, processed 200 new accounts]
-------------------

Draft a high-impact self-performance review paragraph. Use strong, active verbs (e.g., rebuilt, optimized, processed) and focus on the quantifiable metrics provided. Avoid passive statements (like "was responsible for") and corporate fluff (like synergy, seamlessly, foster, testament). Keep it concise, professional, and impact-driven. Limit to 120 words.

### Situation 8: Apologizing Professionally for a Mistake
Role: Accountable Professional
Task: Own a mistake and present corrective steps immediately.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Recipient Name: [Manager/Client Name]
The Mistake: [E.g., sent the outdated slide deck to the stakeholders]
Corrective Steps Taken: [E.g., recalled the email and sent the updated deck]
Preventive Action: [E.g., renamed outdated templates to prevent mix-ups]
-------------------

Draft a direct email addressing the mistake. State what went wrong clearly, own the error without being overly defensive, and immediately detail the corrective and preventive actions. Do NOT use passive voice or cliché phrasing like "apologize for any inconvenience." Keep it professional, constructive, and action-oriented. Under 100 words.

### Situation 9: Responding to Critical Feedback
Role: Constructive Communicator
Task: Acknowledge tough critiques constructively and move forward.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Sender Name: [Manager/Client Name]
Critical Feedback: [E.g., the copywriting draft lacks a clear hook and is too long]
Next Steps: [E.g., cutting the intro by half, starting with the primary statistic]
-------------------

Draft a professional response to the feedback. Acknowledge and validate their critique, then outline the specific next steps to address the concern. Keep the tone collaborative, objective, and action-oriented. Do not make excuses, complain, or write defensive filler. Keep it under 120 words.

### Situation 10: Cold Networking Outreach
Role: Professional Networker
Task: Write a low-pressure cold email requesting a brief informational chat.

--- USER INPUTS ---
Target Name: [Recipient's Name]
Specific Area of Interest: [E.g., their transition from engineering to product management]
Mutual Context (if any): [E.g., both grew up in Boston, or "None"]
-------------------

Draft a low-friction, polite cold email to request a brief informational chat. State why you are reaching out to them specifically, highlight the mutual context, and make a small, easy-to-accept ask (e.g., a 15-minute Zoom call). Keep the tone respectful, curious, and concise. Do not sell anything or ask for a job. Under 100 words.

### Situation 11: OOO Handover Sync
Role: Organized Project Lead

--- USER INPUTS ---

Vacation Dates: [E.g., July 15 to July 20]

Draft a structured OOO handover email. State the dates clearly, list each active project with its designated coverage person, and explain the emergency contact protocol. Keep the format highly scannable (using bullet points) and easy to read from a phone. Limit to 120 words.
