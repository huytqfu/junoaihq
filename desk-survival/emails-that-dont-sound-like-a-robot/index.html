Write Emails and Reports That Don't Sound Like a Robot: The AI Editing SOP

The Juno Desk · 5 min read · Verified guide

AI-generated emails default to "corporate sycophant" mode. Left to itself, your chatbot will write like a Victorian butler trying to hit a word count. Here is the SOP to strip the robotic gloss and write like a human.

There is a specific brand of dread that comes with receiving an email starting with "I hope this email finds you well." We all know the AI wrote it, and we all know you didn't read it. Writing office emails that sound human requires teaching your large language model (LLM) to stop acting like a butler. If your colleagues can tell within the first five words that you ran your status report through a chatbot, you aren't saving time — you are signaling that you couldn't be bothered to write it yourself.

Ed. note Banning the word "delve" is not a suggestion. It is a condition of employment.

Why AI sounds like a robot

AI models write based on statistical probability, predicting the most common word that should follow the previous one. Because the internet is flooded with dry, corporate press releases and generic cover letters, the model defaults to this baseline. This results in distinct robotic tells: excessive politeness, uniform sentence lengths (syntactic symmetry), and vague summaries instead of concrete facts.

Left to their default settings, AI models write completely devoid of human personality.

The AI Cliché Registry (Words to Ban)

To make your AI write like a human, you must first ban the vocabulary that exposes it. Treat these terms as red flags in your prompts:

Robotic Word/Phrase Why It Fails Human Replacement
Delve Nobody says this. "Let's delve into the data" sounds artificial. Look at, review, go over
Testament "This success is a testament to..." is overly dramatic for a status report. Shows, proves, is because of
Tapestry Used by AI to describe any collection of things ("a tapestry of ideas"). Mix, collection, range
Moreover / Furthermore These transitions belong in academic essays, not casual work emails. Also, besides, plus
Please find attached Stiff and old-fashioned. I've attached, here is the
In today's digital landscape A classic filler phrase that adds zero value to a report. Cut entirely
Seamlessly / Meticulously Overused adverbs that try too hard to oversell performance. Cut, or describe the actual process
Foster / Cultivate "To foster collaboration." Real managers say "help people work together." Help, build, grow

The "Tone Blueprint" Framework

The secret to natural AI writing is context framing. If you ask an AI to "write a follow-up email," you will get a generic template. You must provide a four-part Tone Blueprint that defines:

  • Context: What is happening? Why are you writing this right now?
  • Audience: Who is receiving this? What is your relationship with them (peer, client, executive)?
  • Constraints: What should the AI not do? (e.g., no corporate jargon, keep it under 150 words).
  • Reference Text: Paste a short paragraph of your own writing to let the AI analyze and match your style.

Step-by-step SOP for email drafting

Step 1: Gather the raw facts. Do not ask the AI to invent details. Write out the raw, ugly facts in bullet points. Do not worry about grammar or structure yet.

Step 2: Apply constraints. Run the prompt using a structured template that explicitly strips out robotic tells.

Step 3: The sentence variation check. Scan the sentence lengths. Robotic writing has uniform sentence lengths. Human writing has variety. Ensure you have short sentences (3-5 words) mixed with medium ones.

Step 4: The final trope sweep. Do a quick manual sweep to delete any opening clichés like "I hope this email finds you well" or sign-offs like "Should you have any questions..."

Before & After Prompt Templates

Scenario A: The Project Delay Email

When a deadline slips, you need to deliver bad news without sounding defensive or robotic.

You are a senior project lead. Write a short email to a client, Mark, explaining that the project presentation will be delayed by two days. Follow these strict rules: - State the delay and the new deadline in the first sentence. - Give a brief, honest reason (team capacity issue) without over-explaining. - Do NOT use: apologize for any inconvenience, delve, testament, or moreover. - Do NOT start with "I hope this email finds you well" or any greeting clichés. - Use a direct, professional, yet casual tone. Keep it under 100 words. Raw info: - Old deadline: Friday at 5 PM. - New deadline: Tuesday by 10 AM. - Reason: Key designer is out sick, slowing down visual assembly.

Before (Without Rules): "Dear Mark, I hope this email finds you well. I am writing to humbly apologize for any inconvenience, but due to unforeseen circumstances regarding team illness, our presentation is delayed. We are delving into the remaining assets meticulously. Please find the new delivery scheduled for Tuesday at 10 AM. Should you have any questions, do not hesitate to ask."

After (With Rules): "Hi Mark, we need to push our presentation delivery from Friday afternoon to Tuesday morning at 10 AM. One of our lead designers went down with a flu yesterday, which has slowed down our slide assembly. We want to make sure the final layouts are polished before you see them. Thanks for understanding, and let me know if you need to reschedule our walk-through meeting."

Scenario B: The Weekly Status Report

Status reports are often skipped because they read like automated system logs. Make them readable.

You are a marketing manager. Write a bulleted weekly status update for your department head. Rules: - Start with a single sentence highlighting the main win of the week. - Use bullet points for: Wins, Blockers, and Next Steps. - Use conversational language. Write "We fixed" instead of "Resolution was achieved." - Ban these words: seamless, showcase, delve, key, leverage, synergy. - Keep the entire report under 150 words. Raw facts: - Completed the LinkedIn campaign setup. - Budget approved by finance after a 3-day delay. - Blocker: Waiting on copywriter for ad headlines. - Next week: Launch ads, monitor CTR.

Scenario C: The Cold Client Follow-up

Cold outreach must sound human, or it will immediately go to the spam folder.

You are a business development representative. Write a quick follow-up email to a prospect who downloaded a guide from our site but hasn't responded to our first email. Rules: - Keep the entire email under 75 words. - Do NOT pitch the product. Ask a simple, open-ended question about their workflow. - Do NOT use formal transitions or greeting clichés. - Write with the brevity of a text message sent from on a phone. Raw facts: - Prospect: David (Operations Manager). - Resource downloaded: "AI Data Entry Checklist". - Goal: Get him to share his main data entry bottleneck.

The Output:

Hi David, I saw you grabbed our AI Data Entry Checklist last week. Most operations managers we work with use it because their team is losing hours to manual typing errors. Did the checklist help clarify your setup, or are you dealing with a specific bottleneck that wasn't covered? Best, [Name]

Best Practices for Reports

When applying this SOP to longer documents, like monthly performance reports, the same rules apply: lead with data trends, highlight the "why" behind the numbers, and write in the active voice. Using AI to draft your written communications is a powerful way to clear your queue, but the final editing pass belongs to you.
